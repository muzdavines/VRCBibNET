Everyone Did What Was Right
Judges 2–3
The Need for a Righteous King
Intro. The Promises Fulfilled, The People Drifting
Joshua 21:45, Joshua 23:14, Judges 2:10
Opening Arc Connection
We’ve moved from:
      . Abraham receiving the promise
      . Israel was enslaved in Egypt
      . God redeeming His people through Exodus
      . The wilderness generation
      . Joshua leading Israel into the land
And by the end of Joshua something remarkable is said twice.
         “Not one word of all the good promises that the LORD had made to the house of Israel had failed; all came to pass.” Joshua 21:45
Then Joshua says again:
         “You know in your hearts and souls… that not one word has failed of all the good things that the LORD your God promised concerning you.” Joshua 23:14
That matters because Judges opens with collapse.
But the collapse cannot be blamed on God.
The land had been given, promises kept, God had proven covenant faithful.
So what went wrong?
Judges answers with devastating simplicity:
         “And there arose another generation after them who DID NOT KNOW the YHWH or the work that he had done for Israel.” Judges 2:10
This generation knew the stories intellectually, no longer knew the Lord covenantally, personally, obediently.
They inherited blessing while losing the God who gave it.
New Testament scholar D. A. Carson once warned:
         “One generation believes the gospel and the entailments of the gospel. The next
         generation assumes the gospel while focusing on the entailments. The third
         generation denies the gospel altogether while still trying to keep the entailments.”
And honestly, that feels frighteningly modern.
We live in a culture still borrowing Christian morality while abandoning the Christian God underneath it.
Spider-Noir - Saving Grace. - Tommy Robinson 
That is where Judges had landed.
~~Metaphor~~
Like a tree cut from its roots / bouquet of flowers that still appears alive for a season, a culture can retain the appearance of spiritual life long after disconnecting from the source.
Application
The church is never automatically safe.
Faith cannot simply be inherited culturally.
Every generation must know the Lord personally.
Transition
And once covenant faithfulness disappears, the downward spiral begins.
1. The Cycle of Sin
Judges 2:11–19
The Pattern of Judges
This section gives the pattern for nearly the entire book:
Israel sins
God gives them over to enemies
Israel suffers
Israel cries out
God raises a deliverer
Temporary peace
Then the people fall again
It is a spiritual spiral.
And each cycle gets darker.
Israel’s Idolatry
“And the people of Israel did what was evil in the sight of the LORD and served the Baals.”
Why Baal?
Because idols always promise control.
Baal worship promised:
fertility
prosperity
crops
success
security
The temptation was practical.
Trust the invisible covenant God, Or trust what appears immediate and effective?
Human nature has not changed.
Modern idols may not stand carved on hills, but people still worship:
self\
sexuality
success
identity
politics
comfort
reputation
Historical Quote
R. C. Sproul said: “Sin is cosmic treason.”
Judges reminds us that Israel’s idolatry was not merely bad decision-making.
It was rebellion against the holy King who redeemed them from slavery.
The Mercy of God
Even here, grace appears.
V 18: “For the LORD was moved to pity by their groaning…”
That is astonishing.
Israel repeatedly abandons God. Yet God repeatedly shows mercy.
~~Metaphor~~
Sin is like spiritual gravity. Left unchecked, the heart naturally drifts downward.
Application
One of the terrifying realities of sin is how quickly people normalise it.
What once shocked the conscience slowly becomes ordinary.
And Judges feels disturbingly contemporary because modern culture increasingly celebrates what Scripture calls rebellion.
Transition
But even in judgment, God keeps raising deliverers.
And that reveals both His mercy and Israel’s deeper problem.

2. Temporary Saviours
Judges 2:16–18
God Raises Judges
“Then the LORD raised up judges, who saved them…”
The judges are acts of mercy. Israel deserves abandonment.
Instead, God intervenes repeatedly.
But something important becomes clear as the book progresses.

The judges themselves are flawed.

As Judges unfolds:
      . the leaders weaken
      . compromise increases
      .moral confusion spreads
The deliverers begin reflecting the corruption of the people.
That is intentional. The book is teaching us something profound.
Human saviours are not enough.
Historical Quote
Sinclair Ferguson said:
           “The heart of every problem is the problem of the heart.”
Israel’s real issue was not Midianites, Moabites, or Canaanites.
The deepest enemy was within.
~~Metaphor~~
It is like patching cracks in a collapsing wall while the foundation underneath continues crumbling.
The judges provide temporary rescue, but not lasting transformation.
Application
Human leaders always disappoint eventually.
Politics disappoint.
Movements disappoint.
Celebrities disappoint.
Even spiritual leaders fail.
Judges strips away false confidence in human strength.
Transition
And yet God continues showing mercy.
The first glimpse of that mercy appears through Othniel.
3. Othniel and the Mercy of God
Judges 3:7–11
The First Judge
Othniel establishes the pattern:
     . Israel sins
     . God disciplines
     . Israel cries out
     . God raises a deliverer
V3:9: “But when the people of Israel cried out to the LORD, the LORD raised up a deliverer…”
That line matters.
Salvation begins with divine mercy, not human worthiness.
Israel is not rescued because they deserve rescue.
They are rescued because God remains faithful to His covenant promises.

Charles Spurgeon: “God’s mercy is so great that you may sooner drain the sea of its water than deprive His grace of forgiveness.”
Application
That is still true today.
People often imagine they must somehow become worthy before coming to God.
But Scripture repeatedly shows desperate sinners crying out to a merciful God.
Gospel Movement
Judges keeps showing human failure.
But it also keeps showing divine mercy breaking into human ruin.
Transition
And all of this prepares us for the deeper message of the book.

4. Everyone Did What Was Right
Judges as a Whole
The Repeated Line
The line repeated throughout Judges becomes the interpretive key:
“In those days there was no king in Israel. Everyone did what was right in his own eyes.”
That is not freedom. That is anarchy of the soul.
Theological Point
Judges is not mainly about political instability.
It is about covenant disorder.
When people reject God’s rule, they enthrone themselves.
And once self becomes king, chaos follows.
~~Metaphor~~
When every person becomes their own king, society fragments into millions of competing little kingdoms.
Modern Connection
This is why Judges feels so modern.
“Do what feels right.” “Live your truth.” “Define yourself.”“Follow your heart.”
That is simply Judges rewritten in modern language.
Application
Human autonomy sounds liberating.
Autonomous humanity does not move toward righteousness.
It moves toward chaos.
Transition
And that creates longing.
Judges begins making us hunger for something greater.
Not another temporary deliverer, A righteous king.

5. Christ the True and Righteous King
The Longing of Judges
The entire book points beyond itself.
Israel does not merely need:
     . military victories
     . stronger judges
     . better systems
Israel needs righteous rule.
The judges fail repeatedly.
Christ does not.
Contrast
The judges rescue temporarily. Christ saves eternally.

The judges fight external enemies. Christ defeats sin and death.

The judges themselves are flawed. Christ is perfectly righteous.

The judges die. Christ reigns forever.
Historical Quote
Abraham Kuyper declared:
         “There is not a square inch in the whole domain of human existence over which Christ, who is Sovereign over all, does not cry: Mine!”
That is the answer to Judges.
Not self-rule. Christ’s rule.
Gospel Landing
At the cross, Christ bears the judgment rebellious humanity deserves.
The true King wears a crown of thorns.
The righteous Judge is condemned in the place of sinners.
Then in the resurrection He establishes the kingdom that cannot fail.

~Revelation Connection~
Judges shows fractured humanity under sinful rule.
Revelation shows redeemed humanity under the perfect reign of Christ.

Conclusion
Judges is not merely ancient Israelite history.
It is a spotlight on the human condition.

The problem in Judges was never ultimately political, it was spiritual.
The people rejected righteous rule and enthroned themselves instead.
And humanity has been repeating that pattern ever since Eden.
Everyone serves a king. The question is not whether you bow.
The question is who you are bowing to now.
Because self-rule always leads toward another version of Judges.
But Christ is the righteous King humanity truly needs.
So stop doing what is right in your own eyes.
Bow before the King who never fails His people.
And unlike the judges of Israel, His kingdom will never collapse.
