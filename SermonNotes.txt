Genesis 32
A New Name for a New Man
Focus: Transformation through grace
Intro.
• Genesis 12 – God calls Abraham.
 The promise begins: land, seed, blessing to the nations.
• Genesis 22 – Abraham and Isaac on Mount Moriah.
 God preserves the promised son.
 The covenant line continues through Isaac.
• Isaac has Esau and Jacob.
 God declares before they are born:
 “The older will serve the younger.”
• The covenant line will pass through Jacob.
But Jacob’s early life is marked by grasping and deception:
• grasping at birth (Gen 25)
 • deceiving Isaac (Gen 27)
 • stealing the blessing (Gen 27)
 • conflict with Esau (Gen 27)
Result: Jacob flees the land.
• Twenty years later, in Genesis 32, Jacob is travelling back because God told him to return (Gen 31:3).
• Jacob left the land as a fugitive and deceiver.
During those twenty years something else has been happening:
God has been quietly at work.
• Bethel – God promises to bring him back
 • Protection and provision under Laban
 • Family and wealth grow
Yet something in Jacob himself is still unresolved.
Before he can face Esau,
 Before he can stand in the line of promise,
God must deal with the man.
Genesis 32 becomes the turning point in Jacob’s life.
The man who spent his life grasping
 becomes the man who clings to God.
By morning he will carry:
• a new name
 • a limp
 • a transformed identity

1. God’s Presence Before the Crisis
Genesis 32:1–2
Jacob went on his way, and the angels of God met him.
 And when Jacob saw them he said,
 “This is God's camp!”
 So he called the name of that place Mahanaim.
Scene
• Jacob is returning toward the land of promise
 • Suddenly he encounters angels of God
This echoes earlier moments in Jacob’s life.
At Bethel he saw the ladder between heaven and earth.
 Now again heaven breaks into the journey.
Jacob names the place Mahanaim, meaning “two camps.”
Before Esau appears, before the danger arrives,
 God reminds Jacob that he is not travelling alone.

Matthew Henry:
“God often sends comfort to His people before their troubles come, that when the trial arrives they may remember they are not forsaken.”
Application
God often works like this.
“Fear not, for I am with you.” (Isaiah 41:10)
Before a difficult season:
• a word from Scripture
 • an unexpected encouragement
 • a reminder of His presence
At the time it may seem small.
Later we realise God was preparing us for what was coming.

2. The Past Returns
Genesis 32:3–8
Scene
Jacob sends messengers ahead to Esau.
The report comes back.
Esau is coming.
 And he is bringing 400 men.
This is not a friendly family reunion.
Twenty years earlier Esau had sworn:
 “I will kill my brother Jacob.”
Jacob now realises the past he ran from has finally caught up.
Reaction
Verse 7 says:
Jacob was greatly afraid and distressed.
Jacob has seen angels.
 He has heard God’s promises.
 Yet he is still afraid.
Faith does not eliminate fear.
 But faith teaches us where to take our fear.

John Calvin:
“The faithful are often disturbed by fear, but they learn to cast their anxieties upon God.”
Application
Many people carry something similar.
• a past mistake
 • a broken relationship
 • a consequence avoided for years
Eventually the past must be faced.
Jacob cannot run anymore.

3. Jacob’s Prayer
Genesis 32:9–12
The Moment
Jacob finally turns to God.
This is the first deeply personal prayer recorded in his life.
Notice the structure of the prayer.
He remembers who God is
 “The God of my father Abraham and the God of my father Isaac.”
He remembers what God said
 “You said, I will surely do you good.”
Then he confesses something remarkable.
“I am not worthy of the least of all the steadfast love you have shown.”
This is new.
The old Jacob demanded blessing.
 This Jacob recognises grace.

Martin Luther:
“Prayer is not overcoming God’s reluctance, but laying hold of His promise.”
Application
True prayer often begins here.
Not with confidence in ourselves.
But with humility.
We come to God saying:
• You promised
 • I am unworthy
 • I need your mercy
That is the soil where faith grows.

4. Old Habits Still Working
Genesis 32:13–21
Scene
Jacob sends gifts ahead to Esau.
Herd after herd moves forward:
• goats
 • sheep
 • camels
 • cattle
Each servant is instructed to say:
“These belong to your servant Jacob.”
Jacob is attempting to soften Esau’s anger.
The strategy is clear:
Wave after wave of generosity.
Reflection
Something interesting is happening.
Jacob has prayed.
But he is also still planning.
Faith and strategy often live side by side.
 (Think of Moses praying while Joshua fought Amalek – Exodus 17.)
Jacob trusts God, yet he still tries to manage the situation.
But the deeper issue in Jacob’s life is not solved yet.
God is about to deal with that.

Charles Spurgeon:
“Prayer without effort is presumption, and effort without prayer is unbelief.”
Application
Believers often live in this tension.
We pray.
 We plan.
But sometimes God allows our strategies to reach their limit so we learn where the real source of blessing lies.

5. Alone With God
Genesis 32:22–26
Scene
Jacob sends everyone across the Jabbok River.
Then the text gives one simple sentence:
“Jacob was left alone.”
No servants.
 No family.
 No strategies.
Just Jacob.
And God.
Then the struggle begins.
“A man wrestled with him until the breaking of the day.”
This moment mirrors Jacob’s entire life.
He has wrestled with:
• Esau
 • Isaac
 • Laban
Now he wrestles with God Himself.
!! Turning Point !!
God touches Jacob’s hip.
Instantly his strength collapses.
! the text says God touched the socket of Jacob’s hip, not overpowered
! The struggle lasted all night not because God lacked power, but because God was doing something deeper in Jacob.
Yet Jacob refuses to let go.: “I will not let you go unless you bless me.”
The man who once stole blessing
 now clings to God for it.
A. W. Tozer: “It is doubtful whether God can bless a man greatly until He has hurt him deeply.”
Application
Sometimes God breaks the place where we trust ourselves most.
God touches the place that supports Jacob’s strength and movement.
From that moment forward Jacob will walk with a limp.
The man who spent his life striving will now walk forward leaning on God.
Not to destroy us. But to teach us dependence.

6. A New Name
Genesis 32:27–29
God asks a question:
“What is your name?”
The last time Jacob was asked a question about identity, in Genesis 27, he answered deceptively.  he finally speaks the truth: “Jacob.”
Before he can receive the new name, he must first confess the old one.
God is not seeking information.
He is asking Jacob to confront his identity.
Jacob.
The name means:
• deceiver
 • heel grabber
 • supplanter
Then God speaks the words that change everything:
“You shall no longer be called Jacob, but Israel.”
God does not erase Jacob’s past.
He transforms his identity.
Grace does not pretend the past never happened.
Grace transforms the sinner who finally tells the truth about himself.
Augustine: “God loves each of us as if there were only one of us to love.”
God deals with Jacob personally.
Application
This is how grace works.
God doesn’t  merely forgive.
He gives a new identity.
“If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation.”   (2 Corinthians 5:17)

7. Marked by Grace
Genesis 32:30–32
Jacob names the place Peniel.
“I have seen God face to face, and yet my life has been delivered.”
The encounter with God in the night prepares Jacob for the encounter with Esau in the morning.
Then the final image:
“The sun rose upon him… and he was limping.”
New name.  New relationship with God.
But a permanent limp.
In the Old Testament the thigh or hip is sometimes associated with strength and with family lineage.
! the father of the nation Israel is marked at that very place.
But the main point is clear: God breaks Jacob’s strength so that from this moment forward he must walk dependent on Him.
Grace transforms Jacob, but it leaves him dependent.
Augustine: “Our hearts are restless until they rest in You.”
Application
The Christian life often looks like Jacob leaving Peniel.
Forgiven.
 Changed.
 Walking with God.
But still aware of weakness.
 Still dependent on grace.
Conclusion 
Genesis began with promise.
In Genesis 12 God called Abraham and promised:
• a land
 • a people
 • blessing to all nations.
But the story quickly reveals a problem.
The promise moves through very flawed people.
Abraham falters.
 Isaac struggles.
 Jacob is a deceiver and grasping man.
Genesis 32 shows us something crucial.
God does not advance redemption through perfect people.
He advances it through people transformed by grace.
Jacob enters the night grasping.
 He leaves the morning Israel.
The covenant line continues not because Jacob is strong,
 but because God is faithful.
But the story does not stop there.
Jacob becomes Israel.
 Israel becomes a nation.
Yet the same struggle continues throughout their history.
The Old Testament begins pointing forward to one true Israel,
 one faithful Son who would succeed where the nation failed.
That Son is Jesus Christ.
.Jacob wrestled through the night at the Jabbok.
.Jesus wrestled in prayer in Gethsemane.
.Jacob clung to God to receive blessing.
.Jesus submitted to the Father to bear the curse.
.At the cross Christ carried the judgment that grasping sinners deserve.
.Through His resurrection the blessing promised to Abraham is opened to the world.
.The arc of redemption moves like this:
God promised blessing to the nations through Abraham.
 That promise passed through Isaac.
 Then through Jacob, the man who became Israel.
But the promise ultimately reaches its fulfilment in Christ, the true and faithful Son.
And now everyone who comes to Christ by faith receives what Jacob received that night:
• a new standing before God
 • a new identity
 • a new future shaped by grace rather than the past.
Jacob walked away from Peniel with a new name.
Through the gospel God gives something even greater.
He calls sinners His children.
The story that began with one struggling man on the banks of the Jabbok now extends to people from every nation who are brought into the family of God through Christ.
The man who spent his life grasping was transformed by grace.
And that is still how God works today.
He meets sinners in the dark places where their strength fails, and He gives them a new name, a new identity, and a new life in Christ.
Next time we leave Genesis and move into Exodus, where the people who bear the name Israel will soon find themselves enslaved in Egypt, and God will once again show that He is the One who saves His people.
