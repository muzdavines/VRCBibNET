The Rejected King
Biblical Arc Series
Scripture: 1 Samuel 8-10
Focus: The True King Rejected
The Arc So Far
20th  sermon in our Biblical Arc series, pausing to see where the story has brought us.
We began in Eden ~ humanity created in God's image and then fall into sin.
We heard the first promise of redemption in Genesis 3:15, that one day the Seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head.
Since then, every stage of the story has been answering one question:
How will God bring about that promised Deliverer?
We followed the promise through Noah.
Through Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.
Through Joseph.
We saw God redeem His people through Moses and the Exodus, ee received the Law at Sinai, we crossed the Jordan with Joshua.
Then we watched the tragic downward spiral of Judges. 
Again and again [God's people rebelled |  God rescued them | fell back into sin]
The book of Judges ended with a chilling summary: "Everyone did what was right in his own eyes."
That naturally raises a question:

What is Israel missing?
3 great Old Testament offices that ultimately point us to Christ.
.       In Moses we saw the anticipation of the Prophet.
.       In Boaz we saw the anticipation of the Redeemer, pointing us toward the priestly work of Christ.
.       In Samuel, the spotlight falls upon the King.
The Bible is progressively answering three questions:
Who is the true Prophet / Redeemer / King?
And the answer to all three questions is Jesus Christ.
.       The Prophet who perfectly reveals God.
.       The Redeemer who saves His people.
.       The King who reigns forever.
That is why today's passage is so important.
The story is no longer simply moving toward a people.
The story is moving toward a throne.
.      Who will rule God's people?
.      Who will lead them?
.      Who will save them?
.      Who will be their king?
At first glance, 1 Samuel 8 appears to provide the answer.
Israel asks for a king, but what follows is one of the great tragedies of Scripture.
The people reject the King they already have.
Intro: The Crown Jeweller
Imagine a master crown jeweller presenting a king with a magnificent crown.
It is crafted from pure gold. Every jewel is genuine. Every detail is perfect. It is priceless.


The king examines it briefly, places it on the table, and points to another crown in the corner.
"Actually, I'd rather have that one."


The jeweller is horrified.
The other crown merely looks impressive. shines,catches the eye – an imitation. 
Inferior materials lie beneath its polished surface.

Most people would think such a king had lost his senses.
Yet that is exactly what Israel does in 1 Samuel 8.
God Given Himself - infinitely valuable. The Lord was their King.
Egypt,wilderness, enemies, remained faithful despite their repeated rebellion.
 
Israel looked at the surrounding nations  "We want what they have."
The tragedy of this chapter is not that Israel chooses a bad king over a good king. exchanging the perfect King for an imperfect substitute.


By the end of the passage we will discover that Israel's rejection points us forward to another King, one who would also be rejected, but whose kingdom would never end.

{Read 1 Samuel 8:1-9}
1. Israel Rejects the Priceless Crown
Samuel is old. Sons, Joel and Abijah, have not followed in his ways. They take bribes and pervert justice.
The elders come to Samuel with what sounds like a reasonable request:
.           "Now appoint for us a king to judge us like all the nations."
At first glance it sounds sensible.
Samuel is ageing , sons are corrupt -nation needs stability.
But God sees beneath the request. 2ce the emphasis falls on being "like all the nations."
.      Israel was never called to be like the nations.
.      They were called to belong to God.
The Lord's response reveals the true issue:
.       "They have not rejected you, but they have rejected me from being king over them."
The crown is already on the rightful King's head. The throne is already occupied.
Israel is not solving a leadership problem.
They are rejecting God's rule.
Like the king in our opening illustration, they are pushing aside the priceless crown because another one appears more attractive.
This is one of the great tragedies of the human heart.
As C. S. Lewis observed, “if we will not worship God, we will worship something else.”
Human beings never stop serving a king. Israel would not live without a king.
They no longer wanted the King they already had.
The same temptation remains today.
We may not ask for a king named Saul, but we constantly place other things on the throne of our lives.
.      Success.
.      Security.
.      Comfort.
.      Approval.
.      Politics.
.      Money.
Even ourselves.
The question is never whether we have a king. 
The question is which king we serve.
{Read 1 Samuel 8:10-18}
2. The Cheap Imitation Comes With Hidden Costs
God tells Samuel to warn the people.
Sober warning- the king will take their.
.     sons
.     daughters.
.     fields.
.     vineyards.
.     servants.
The repeated theme is simple:
Take, take, take, take.
The imitation crown shines brightly in the display cabinet.
But underneath lies disappointment.
Israel sees prestige. God sees oppression.
Israel sees security. God sees bondage.
Sin always works this way.
The advertisement looks better than the reality.
The promise sounds bigger than the delivery.
The idol always costs more than expected.
The people imagine a king who will give.
God warns them that their king will take.
False kings always demand more than they give.
.    Money promises security.
.    Success promises fulfilment.
.    Power promises significance.
Yet none of them can bear the weight we place upon them.
Whenever we exchange God for something else, we eventually discover that the imitation cannot do what only the genuine King can do.
{Read 1 Samuel 8:19-22}
3. The People Prefer Appearance Over Value
Despite the warning, the people refuse to listen.
.              "No! But there shall be a king over us."
It is one of the saddest moments in Israel's history.
God has spoken clearly. The consequences have been explained.
The dangers have been exposed.
Yet the people have already made up their minds.
Their response reveals what is really driving them:
.           "That we also may be like all the nations, and that our king may judge us and go out before us and fight our battles."
Again!   "We want to be like the nations."
That phrase is the heartbeat of Israel's rebellion.
For centuries God had been teaching Israel that they were different.
Not because they were [better | stronger | more numerous]
But because God had chosen them and set His covenant love upon them.
Their calling was never to blend in. Their calling was to stand out.
When the nations looked at Israel, they were supposed to see something different.
They were supposed to see a people whose God was among them.
A people whose [law came from God | worship centred upon God | hope rested in God]
Yet now Israel looks at the nations and says: "We would rather be like them."
This temptation has never disappeared, we constantly face the same pressure.
The world says: "Be like us.", “think like us”. “value what we value”, “pursue what we pursue”, “Measure success the way we measure success.
Yet God's people have always been called to swim against the current.
The pressure to conform is powerful.
It was powerful in Samuel's day, it is powerful in ours.
The problem is that Israel is judging success by appearance.
They look around and see kings, armies, palaces, and military power.
They see what appears impressive.
What they fail to see is that those nations are spiritually bankrupt, 
.       evaluating by sight rather than by faith.
And that brings us to the 2nd reason they want a king:
.         "That our king may go out before us and fight our battles."
That statement should stop us in our tracks.
Who had been fighting Israel's battles all along?
Who defeated Pharaoh and his armies at the Red Sea?
Who brought down the walls of Jericho?
Who delivered Midian into Gideon's hands?
Who defeated Sisera?
Who rescued Israel throughout the period of the judges?
The answer is always the same.

The Lord.
.     Again and again God fought for His people.
.     Again and again God delivered them.
.     Again and again God proved Himself faithful.
Yet Israel now places its confidence in a man.
They are looking to Saul to do what God has already been doing.
This is what unbelief often looks like, It is not always shaking a fist at heaven.
More often it is quietly transferring our trust from God to something else.
Faith is not merely believing God exists. Faith is believing God is enough.
Israel knew God existed. Their problem was that they no longer trusted Him.
They preferred what they could see.And that brings us to Saul.

{Read 1 Samuel 9:1-2, 15-17; 10:1}
4. God Gives Them What They Demand
Saul enters the story exactly as Israel would have wanted.
.   He is handsome, tall, “stands head and shoulders” above everyone else,  He looks like a king.
If Israel were choosing a king based on appearance alone, Saul would be the obvious choice.
He is the crown that catches everyone's eye in the jeweller's window.
Bright, Impressive, Visually appealing.
Yet while Israel sees Saul's appearance, God is quietly directing events behind the scenes.
The Lord reveals Saul to Samuel. The Lord appoints Saul. The Lord anoints Saul.
Even in Israel's rebellion, God remains sovereign.

That is important!!
Israel's rejection does not derail God's plan.
God is never reacting, God is always ruling.
Yet there is also a warning here.
Sometimes God's judgment comes not through refusal but through permission.
Israel insists, God grants the request.
The king they demand is the king they receive.
Sometimes the most frightening answer God can give is "yes."
{Read 1 Samuel 10:17-24}
5. The Rejected King Points to the True King
Before Saul is publicly presented, Samuel gathers the nation together.
Notice what Samuel does, he doesn’t begin with Saul. he begins with God.
.    He reminds the people of God's faithfulness.
.    God brought them out of Egypt.
.    God delivered them from slavery.
.    God rescued them from their enemies.
..God preserved them throughout their history.


Then comes the indictment:
.       "But today you have rejected your God."
Those words explain everything.
The issue was never simply the establishment of a monarchy.
The issue was the rejection of God's kingship.

Before Saul ever receives public acclaim, Samuel wants the nation to understand exactly what has happened.
The crown jeweller's masterpiece has been set aside.
The priceless crown has been rejected.
Yet then Saul appears.
And from a human perspective he looks perfect.
.       "There is none like him among all the people."
That is exactly what Israel wanted to hear.
He looks the part, he sounds the part, he appears strong, he appears capable, he appears kingly.

The crowd erupts in celebration:
.        "Long live the king!"
But the reader already senses the tension.
Everything is focused upon outward appearance,  focused upon visible strength, focused upon human ability.

The people are celebrating the very thing that God warned them about.
And here the story begins to point us beyond Saul.
The entire Old Testament creates a growing longing for a better king.
Saul will fail, David, despite his greatness, will fail, Solomon will fail.
The divided kingdoms will fail, N kingdom (Judah) will fall, S kingdom (Israel) will fall.
One king after another will demonstrate the same truth.
No merely human king can solve humanity's deepest problem.
The throne remains empty of the King God's people truly need.
Then, centuries later, Jesus arrives, the contrast could hardly be greater.
Saul comes from a respected family.
Jesus is born in a manger, 
Saul is physically impressive. Jesus appears ordinary.
Saul stands head and shoulders above the crowd, Jesus takes the form of a servant.
Saul gains a royal crown.    Jesus receives a crown of thorns.
Saul is welcomed by the people.  Jesus is rejected by the people.
Saul is chosen because he looks kingly. Jesus is rejected because He does not fit people's expectations.

Isaiah had foretold it centuries earlier:
.        "He had no form or majesty that we should look at him, and no beauty that we should desire him."

Humanity once again prefers appearance over value.
Once again the priceless crown is pushed aside.
Once again people choose according to sight rather than faith.
Yet here is the great difference.
Saul's story moves from promise to failure. 
Jesus' story moves from rejection to triumph.
The rejected King becomes the crucified King. The crucified King becomes the risen King.
The risen King becomes the enthroned King.

And unlike Saul, Jesus perfectly fulfils everything a king should be.

Saul could not save his people from their sins.
Jesus can, Saul eventually brought disappointment.
Jesus brings eternal life. Saul's kingdom ended.
Jesus' kingdom will never end.
Saul took from his people. Jesus gave Himself for His people.
Saul's reign pointed beyond itself.Jesus is the destination to which the entire story points.
The king Israel needed was never Saul. The king humanity needs is Christ.

Every faithful king in Scripture whispers His name.
Every failed king creates longing for His arrival.
Every throne in Israel's history ultimately points forward to the throne occupied by Jesus Christ, the King of kings and Lord of lords.
Conclusion
Return with me to the jeweller's workshop.
Two crowns remain on the table.
One is genuine. The other is merely an imitation.
One possesses lasting value. The other only sparkles for a season.
Israel chose the imitation.

They exchanged the rule of the living God for the rule of a man.
History proved God's warning true.
Saul failed. The monarchy fractured, The kingdom divided.
Earthly kings came and went. Egypt's Pharaohs are gone.
Assyria's kings are gone. Babylon's emperors are gone.
Rome's Caesars are gone. 
Every kingdom that once appeared permanent has faded into history.
Yet the kingdom of Christ continues to advance.

The rejected King still reigns.
Every heart has a throne. Every life has a king.
The question is not whether you serve a king.
The question is whether your king can save you.
Saul could not. Wealth cannot. Success cannot. Politics cannot.Self cannot.
Only Jesus can.

The King whom Israel ultimately longed for.
The King who wore a crown of thorns before wearing the crown of glory.
The King who was rejected so that sinners might be accepted.
The King who died, rose again, and now reigns forever.
Will we settle for the imitation crowns of this world?
Or will we bow before the true King?
For only one King is worthy.
Only one King conquered death.
Only one King reigns forever.
And His name is Jesus Christ.
Amen.
