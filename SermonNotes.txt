David: The Covenant and the Crown
2 Samuel 7
David's Son and Lord
Introduction
Today is Father's Day.
For many people, Father's Day causes us to think about inheritance, legacy, and what is passed from one generation to the next.
A father may pass [on a trade | a treasured possession.
A farmer may hand over the farm. A builder may hand over the business.
In a monarchy, a crown is passed from one ruler to another.
The crown remains, The heads beneath it change.
One king dies, Another receives the crown.
Then he dies., Then another receives it.
Generation after generation.

~ Slide ~	
Last time we watched Israel receive her first king.
Saul was [selected|anointed|crowned]
He looked every inch a king.
Tall, Impressive, Exactly what the people wanted.

Yet we discovered that outward appearance is not enough. The crown sat upon Saul's head, but obedience did not reign in Saul's heart.
By the end of Saul's story we discover that Israel's problem is deeper than finding the wrong king.
The problem is that every merely human king eventually fails.
Israel doesn't simply need a better king. Israel needs God's King.

~ Slide ~
That brings us to David.
David is a far better king than Saul.
.       He is a man after God's own heart.
.       He shepherds God's people.
.       He loves God's Word.
.       He desires God's glory.
Yet even David is not the destination.
David's greatest contribution to redemptive history is not just that he ruled Israel faithfully.
It is that through David,  God promises the King who will finally wear the crown forever.
Today we arrive at one of the mountain peaks of Scripture.
A chapter so important that everything after it echoes its promises.
Because in 2 Samuel 7 God makes a covenant with David, and that covenant points us beyond David, beyond Solomon, beyond every king of Judah, to Jesus Christ, David's Son and David's Lord.
Big Idea:
The crown passed through David's line for generations, but it finally comes to rest upon the head of the King whose reign will never end.

{Read 2 Samuel 7:1-7}
1. David's Good Desire and God's Greater Plan
The chapter opens during a time of peace.
David is established as king. Jerusalem is secure.
The enemies around Israel have largely been subdued.
For the first time in years David has space to reflect.
And what troubles him is not his own comfort. It is God's honour.
He says to Nathan: "See now, I dwell in a house of cedar, but the ark of God dwells inside tent curtains."
David lives in a palace, God's Ark remains in a tent.
His concern is understandable, Noble

Nathan initially responds: "Go, do all that is in your heart, for the LORD is with you."


But that very night God speaks, And God's answer is unexpected.
David will not build the Temple.At first that seems strange.
After all, David's desire appears entirely reasonable.

Yet God is teaching David a lesson that runs throughout Scripture.
David is thinking about what he can do for God.
God is about to speak about what He will do for David.

The initiative immediately changes direction.
David wants to build a house for God.
God says, in effect:  "David, I have something greater in mind."
The Gospel always works this way.
Human religion asks: "What can I do for God?"
Biblical faith begins with: "What has God done for me?"

Salvation is not about humanity climbing up to God.
It is about God coming down to humanity.
It is grace from beginning to end. 
David is thinking about a building, God is thinking about a kingdom.
David is thinking about a temple, God is thinking about a crown.
David sees the next step,  God sees the next thousand years.
As one commentator notes: "The Bible is not primarily the story of men seeking God, but of God seeking men."
David brings a good plan. God reveals a greater one.
And often that is true in our own lives, We bring our plans before the Lord.
Sometimes He says yes. Sometimes He says no.
But always His purposes are wiser than ours.

{Read 2 Samuel 7:8-11}
2. God Builds a House for David
Before giving the promise, God reminds David where he came from.
"I took you from the sheepfold, from following the sheep, to be ruler over My people."
David was not self-made, Everything he possesses comes from God's grace.
Notice how often God says "I".
.       I took you.
.       I have been with you.
.       I have cut off your enemies.
.       I will make your name great.
.       I will appoint a place.
.       I will give you rest.

David's story is ultimately God's work.
The shepherd became king because God chose him.
The giant fell because God strengthened him.
The kingdom stands because God established it.
Then comes the astonishing promise. "The LORD tells you that He will make you a house."
David wanted to build God a house.

God says: "No David. I will build you one."
Not a palace, or temple.
A dynasty, a royal line,  A covenant family.
This is where our crown metaphor becomes important.
God is not promising David a building,  He is promising successors.
The crown will pass from father to son,
David to Solomon, Solomon to Rehoboam, Rehoboam to the kings of Judah.
Generation after generation.


The crown keeps moving, The promise keeps waiting.
The covenant keeps looking forward.
And if we step back, we can see God's plan narrowing.
In Genesis 3 God promised the Seed of the woman.
In Genesis 12 the Seed of Abraham.
In Genesis 49 the ruler from Judah.
Now in 2 Samuel 7 the royal family is identified.
The Messiah will come through David, The lens of Scripture keeps focusing more sharply.
The promises are converging on one Person.
{Read 2 Samuel 7:12-16}
3. The Son Who Is Coming
This is the heart of the chapter.
God says: "I will set up your seed after you."
Part of this promise clearly refers to Solomon.
Solomon would [follow David |  build the Temple | reign over Israel]


But almost immediately the language becomes larger than Solomon.
God says: "I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever."
Forever!! That creates a problem.
Solomon died, his successors died,The kingdom divided, Jerusalem fell, The people went into exile.


If this promise refers only to Solomon then it ultimately fails.
But God's promises never fail.
Therefore Solomon cannot be the final fulfilment.
He is only a shadow | preview | signpost

Solomon receives the crown. Yet Solomon cannot fulfil the promise.
He sins.He dies.
The crown moves again. Another king comes.
Then another.Then another.
Some good,  Most bad, All mortal.
The crown keeps moving because every king keeps dying.
The Old Testament becomes a long procession of funerals.
The crown passes from one head to another.
Yet none of them is the promised King.


Matthew Henry wrote: "The covenant of royalty made with David and his seed was a figure of the covenant of redemption made with Christ."

2 Kings 25:7: “They slaughtered the sons of Zedekiah before his eyes, and put out the eyes of Zedekiah and bound him in chains and took him to Babylon. 


Eventually Babylon comes, Jerusalem falls.
The throne is emptied,  the crown appears lost.
Yet God has not forgotten His covenant.


Centuries later an angel appears to a young woman named Mary and says:
"The Lord God will give Him the throne of His father David. And He will reign over the house of Jacob forever."
Gabriel is speaking the language of 2 Samuel 7.
The promised Son has arrived, The covenant is being fulfilled.
God has remembered every word He spoke.
As Paul says:"When the fullness of the time had come, God sent forth His Son."
Not early.Not late.Exactly on time.

4. David's Son Is Also David's Lord
Now we reach the great surprise.
The Jews expected Messiah to be David's Son.
What they struggled to understand was that Messiah would also be David's Lord.
Psalm 110 begins: "The LORD said to my Lord, 'Sit at My right hand.'"
Jesus later asks: "If David then calls Him 'Lord,' how is He his Son?"

It is a devastating question.
How can David's descendant be greater than David?
How can David call his own son "Lord"?

Because the coming King is more than merely human.
He is David's Son according to the flesh. He is David's Lord according to His divine nature.
The covenant suddenly becomes bigger than a royal family tree.
The coming King is not merely David's descendant.
He is David's Lord.
The crown belongs to One greater than David himself.
J.I. Packer famously wrote: "The Almighty appeared on earth as a helpless human baby."
The eternal King entered His own creation.
The Creator became part of creation.
The Lord of David became David's Son.
Every previous king inherited the crown from his father.
This King possesses the crown by divine right.
He is not simply another king in the line.
He is the King.
Read Acts 2:29-36
5. The Crown Is Christ's
Peter's sermon at Pentecost ties everything together.
Peter reminds the crowd that David [died|was buried|His tomb remained among them]
Therefore David could not ultimately be speaking about himself, Instead David spoke prophetically about Christ.
Then comes the great turning point. The resurrection.


Every king before Jesus received the crown and eventually surrendered it to death.
Jesus received the crown and conquered death.
Every previous king's reign ended at a grave.

Jesus left the grave behind and continues reigning.
Peter concludes: "God has made this Jesus, whom you crucified, both Lord and Christ."
The covenant reaches its fulfilment, The crown finally comes to rest.
No successor is needed, no replacement is coming, No vacancy will ever occur.

A.W. Tozer once remarked: "Christ has no successor because He has no predecessor."
David's throne has found its final occupant.

And notice that Christ's reign is not merely future.

He reigns now.
King over [His church |  history | nations | rulers  every authority]
Christ is King!
The question is whether we will bow before the King who already reigns.

Conclusion: The Better Son
On Father's Day we naturally think about what fathers pass on to their children.
Through the centuries the crown passed from father to son.
David handed it to Solomon,Solomon handed it to another.
Generation after generation received the covenant promise.
Yet every king eventually died.
Every funeral created another vacancy.
Every crown eventually rested on another head.
Until Jesus.
The Son of David.
The Son of God.
The King who conquered death.
The King who ascended to the Father's right hand.
The King who wears the crown today.
The King who will wear it tomorrow.
The King who will wear it forever.
Take 1 : God's covenant rests upon His grace, not our achievements.
Take 2 : God always keeps His promises.
Take 3 : Jesus is the promised Son of David.
Take 4 : Jesus is also David's Lord.
Take 5 :The King is reigning now and calls us to repentance and faith.

The crown no longer passes from father to son.
The crown rests upon Christ.
The King who wore a crown of thorns now wears the crown of glory.
The King rejected by men now reigns at the Father's right hand.
And one day every knee will bow and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.
Amen.
