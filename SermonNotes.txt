The Test of Faith
Genesis 22
Isaac and the Foreshadowing of Christ
Introduction
Last time we stood in the warmth of covenant promise.
God had taken Abram outside and said, “Look toward heaven.” Count the stars. So shall your offspring be.
Years passed. Waiting. Doubt. Hagar. Ishmael.
Then laughter. Isaac. The child of promise finally arrives.
The covenant seems secure. And then Genesis 22 opens with a terrifying sentence that lands like thunder:  “After these things God tested Abraham.”
The promise is not revoked, tested.
The flood tested the world.Now the promise tests the patriarch.
What happens when the gift God gives becomes the thing He asks you to surrender?

GENESIS 22 – THE MOUNTAIN OF TESTING
1. Take Your Son
Genesis 22:1–2: “Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love…”
After these things God tested Abraham and said to him, “Abraham!” And he said, “Here I am.” 2 He said, “Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go to the land of Moriah, and offer him there as a burnt offering on one of the mountains of which I shall tell you.”
The language tightens.
>Your son.
>Your only son.
>Whom you love.
God names the attachment before He names the altar.
And the command is unthinkable: “Offer him.”
This is not cruelty - testing.
The Hebrew carries the sense of proving. Refining. Revealing.
Not God learning - Abraham is
The promise had come through Isaac, now the promise appears to require Isaac’s death.
>If the promise rests on Isaac’s survival, it will collapse.
>If the promise rests on God’s faithfulness, it will stand.
Metaphor
A foundation only proves its strength under weight. Without pressure, it’s assumption, under pressure, it is revealed.
Quote: Calvin observed that faith is not mere persuasion of the mind, but a firm and effectual confidence in God’s promise, even when circumstances contradict it.
Application:  God doesn’t  test us at the edges, He tests us at the centre, ot to destroy faith, but to detach it from idols, even good ones.
Sometimes the promise itself becomes the rival to the Promiser, “a rival God”

2. The Three Day Journey
Genesis 22:3–8
3 So Abraham rose early in the morning, saddled his donkey, and took two of his young men with him, and his son Isaac. And he cut the wood for the burnt offering and arose and went to the place of which God had told him. 4 On the third day Abraham lifted up his eyes and saw the place from afar. 5 Then Abraham said to his young men, “Stay here with the donkey; I and the boy[a] will go over there and worship and come again to you.” 6 And Abraham took the wood of the burnt offering and laid it on Isaac his son. And he took in his hand the fire and the knife. So they went both of them together. 7 And Isaac said to his father Abraham, “My father!” And he said, “Here I am, my son.” He said, “Behold, the fire and the wood, but where is the lamb for a burnt offering?” 8 Abraham said, “God will provide for himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son.” So they went both of them together.
Abraham rises early.
>No argument recorded.
>No delay.
>No bargaining (as in Genesis 18)
3 days of walking toward what feels like the death of hope.
On the third day he lifts his eyes and sees the place, Isaac carrying the wood, Ab the knife and fire.
Question: “Where is the lamb?”
Abraham’s answers,“God will provide for Himself the lamb.”
Hebrews 11:17-19 tells us Abraham reasoned that God could raise the dead.
17 By faith Abraham, when he was tested, offered up Isaac, and he who had received the promises was in the act of offering up his only son, 18 of whom it was said, “Through Isaac shall your offspring be named.” 19 He considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead, from which, figuratively speaking, he did receive him back.
Resurrection hope before resurrection history.
Metaphor
Faith walks toward the mountain with tears in its eyes but promise in its heart.
Quote
John Owen wrote that “unbelief refuses to rest in God’s sufficiency”. Abraham does the opposite, He rests in it when nothing visible supports it.
Application
We often demand clarity before obedience. Abraham gives obedience before clarity.
Faith isn’t understanding everything, it’s trusting Someone.

3.. The Bound Son
Genesis 22:9–10
9 When they came to the place of which God had told him, Abraham built the altar there and laid the wood in order and bound Isaac his son and laid him on the altar, on top of the wood. 10 Then Abraham reached out his hand and took the knife to slaughter his son. 
>They reach the place.
  >Abraham builds the altar.
  >Arranges the wood.
  >Binds Isaac.
Isaac isn’t a baby, he’s strong enough to carry timber uphill & Abraham is over a hundred years old.
But Isaac submits. The lingering image of this event Jewish tradition calls it “the bibnding”
A willing son, a knife.
Here the shadow comes into focus: Isaac is not a sin bearer,  he is a silhouette.
A beloved son carrying wood up a mountain / A son submitting to his father’s will.
Metaphor: The mountain holds its breath.
Application: True faith surrenders what it cannot understand into the hands of the God it does.

4. The LORD Will Provide
Genesis 22:11–14
11 But the angel of the Lord called to him from heaven and said, “Abraham, Abraham!” And he said, “Here I am.” 12 He said, “Do not lay your hand on the boy or do anything to him, for now I know that you fear God, seeing you have not withheld your son, your only son, from me.” 13 And Abraham lifted up his eyes and looked, and behold, behind him was a ram, caught in a thicket by his horns. And Abraham went and took the ram and offered it up as a burnt offering instead of his son. 14 So Abraham called the name of that place, “The Lord will provide”;[b] as it is said to this day, “On the mount of the Lord it shall be provided.”
At the moment of the raised knife: Heaven intervervenes: “Abraham, Abraham.”
> This is no ordinary angel. He speaks as God, swears as God, and bears divine authority. (11-12)
Then carefully add:
Many Christians have understood this as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ, though the text itself does not explicitly say so.

A ram caught in a thicket, “Instead of his son.”
THERE IT IS!!. Substitution.
Not metaphorical,Literal: The ram dies so Isaac lives.
And Abraham names the place: “The LORD will provide.”
Not just has provided. Will provide.
This location matters its Mt Mariah
Later the site of the temple.
Later still, the region where the greater sacrifice will occur.
Where Isaac is spared, Christ is not.
Where a ram is substituted, Christ becomes the substitute, no angels rescue.
Historic voice
Augustine saw in this text not merely obedience, but prophecy. The son carrying wood is a figure pointing beyond himself.
Application
If you ever doubt the pattern of Scripture, look at this mountain, Genesis is already whispering the gospel.

FROM MORIAH TO CALVARY
At Moriah:
> A father prepared to give his son.
> A substitute provided.
> A covenant reaffirmed.
At Calvary:
> The Father gives His Son.
> No substitute replaces Him.
> He is the substitute.
Paul deliberately this: “He who did not spare His own Son…”
The language is too close to ignore: The test Abraham passed in figure,
God fulfilled in reality.
The knife stopped for Isaac, not for Christ.
> the promise survived the altar in Genesis 22.
> The promise triumphed through the cross in the Gospels.

Covenant Reaffirmed
Genesis 22:15–18
After the test, God swears an oath: “In your offspring shall all the nations of the earth be blessed.”
> Not merely Isaac, ultimately Christ.
Genesis 22 isn’t about heroic parenting.
It is about unstoppable redemption.
The seed will come: The nations will be blessed, Death will not win.

Conclusion
Genesis 22 shows us what faith looks like when tested.
Calvary shows us what love looks like when completed.
> Abraham trusted God to provide a lamb.
> God provided the Lamb.
The test of faith today is not whether we would raise a knife, it is whether we will trust the Father who did not spare His Son.
If you are clinging to something as your security, your identity, your future, your promise, lay it down.
Not because God delights in loss, but because He has already given something greater.
The mountain of testing points to the hill of redemption.
We don’t climb to secure our name, we bow beneath the Name already given.
Trust the One who provided.
