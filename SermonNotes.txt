The Snake in the Wilderness
Text: Num 21:4–9
 Focus: Look and live – John 3:14–15
Introduction: When the Journey Exposes the Heart
Israel is not in Egypt anymore.
They are free. They have:
      . seen the Red Sea open.
      . walked through on dry ground.
      . watched Pharaoh’s army collapse behind them.
      . eaten bread that appeared with the morning dew.
      . They have drunk water from a rock.
And yet here we are again.
“From Mount Hor they set out by the way to the Red Sea… and the people became impatient on the way.” (v4)
That word “impatient” “short of soul”. Their inner life is shrinking under pressure.
And the wilderness does that.  wilderness doesn’t create sin, It reveals it.
You can hide things in comfort that you cannot hide in pressure.
And what comes out of Israel here is not just frustration.
It is something much deeper.
Not Geography, theology
They are not just struggling with the journey. They are struggling with God.

1. The Sin: When the Heart Turns on God
Num 21:4–5
“And the people spoke against God and against Moses…”
That phrase is loaded,  They are not just complaining to Moses, they are speaking against God.
Listen to what they say:
“Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness?”
That is a complete reinterpretation of redemption.
God delivered them from slavery, and they now describe it as a death sentence.
This is what sin does. It rewrites reality, it takes God’s mercy and reframes it as cruelty.
Then they say: “There is no food and no water, and we loathe this worthless food.”
That line almost jars when you read it. “There is no food… and we loathe this food.”
God has been feeding them every single day, faithfully. Consistently. Miraculously.
And now they call it worthless.
~Exegesis~
This is not a supply issue ~ heart issue.
They are not lacking provision, they are rejecting the form in which God provides.
They do not want manna, they want control.
They do not want dependence, they want comfort on their own terms.
~Metaphor~
It is like someone standing under a running tap saying, “I’m dying of thirst,” because they don’t like the taste of the water.
The problem is not the provision.  The problem is the posture.
~Quote~
Augustine said:  “Sin is the soul curved in on itself.”
That is Israel here. Everything is being measured by how it feels to them.
~Application~
This is uncomfortably close to us.
We don’t call it rebellion.
We call it:
    . “I’m just having a hard time.”
    . “I’m just being honest.”
    . “I’m just frustrated.”
But when that frustration turns into accusation, when we begin to question whether God has been good to us,
we are standing in the same place as Israel.
We may have different circumstances. But the same heart.

2. The Judgment: When Sin Is Exposed as Deadly
Num 21:6
“Then the LORD sent fiery serpents among the people…”
The serpents come into the camp.
They bite. And people begin to die.
The text is stark. There is no softening.
~Exegesis~ 
These are likely desert serpents whose bite produces an intense burning sensation.
The word “fiery” reflects that effect.
This is not symbolic language ~ its physical, visible judgment.
And notice this carefully: God doesn’t invent something new, He gives them over to something already present in the wilderness. The danger was always there.
Now it is unleashed.
~Metaphor~
Sin is like venom in the bloodstream:
At first, you may not feel the full effect.
But it is working. Spreading. Corrupting. Weakening.
Until eventually, it reaches the point where you cannot ignore it.
~Quote~
John Owen again, because it fits so well: “Be killing sin, or it will be killing you.”
~Application~
We live in a time where sin is constantly minimised: We rename it.  redefine it. We explain it away.
But Scripture refuses to do that. Sin is not slightly damaging.
It is deadly. Not just socially. Not just emotionally. Eternally.
And here is the uncomfortable truth:
    . The serpents in Num 21 are not the real problem.
    .They reveal the real problem.
    .The real problem is already inside the camp.

3. The Confession: When the Mask Drops
Num 21:7 ~ “And the people came to Moses and said, ‘We have sinned…’”
This is the turning point. not because the snakes stop, but because the people finally see clearly.
They say: “We have spoken against the LORD and against you.”
They call it out without deflection, excuses, soft language, just truth.
~Exegesis~ 
This is one of the clearest examples of corporate confession in the wilderness narratives.
They acknowledge both vertical sin, against the Lord, and horizontal sin, against Moses.
~Metaphor~
It is like someone finally agreeing with the diagnosis.
The illness hasn’t gone away yet.
But now there is a pathway to healing.
~Quote~
Martin Luther:“God creates out of nothing. Therefore, until a man is nothing, God can make nothing out of him.”
Confession is that moment of becoming nothing.
~Application~
This is where the gospel begins to press in.
Because many people will come this far, will admit weakness,  struggle, they will stop short of calling it sin.
And until sin is named,  grace is not understood.
You cannot be saved from something you refuse to acknowledge.

4. The Provision: God’s Surprising Way of Salvation
Num 21:8–9 “Make a fiery serpent and set it on a pole…”
This is one of the most unusual moments in the Old Testament.
God does not remove the serpents; He provides a means of healing in the midst of the judgment.
Moses makes a bronze serpent, lifts it up, and the command is simple: Look.
~Exegesis~ 
This is crucial, The people are still bitten. The poison is still in their bodies.
The situation has not changed externally.
What changes is where they look.
And God attaches a promise to that act of looking.
“When he sees it, shall live.”
~Metaphor~
Imagine someone collapsing from a snake bite. Weak. Fading. Dying.
And the instruction is not: Run, Climb,  Prove yourself.
But simply: Look.
~Quote~
Spurgeon again: :“There is life for a look at the Crucified One.”
~Application~
This offends our instincts deeply.
Because everything in us wants to contribute.
We want to fix it, improve, earn.
God says: Look.
Salvation is not achieved, It is received.

5. The Fulfilment: Christ Lifted Up for Sinners
John 3:14–15
Jesus removes all doubt about what this means.
“As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up…”
That word “must” matters.os not optional the plan of God.
~Exegesis~ 
The serpent was lifted up as the visible focus of faith.
Christ is lifted up on the cross as the final object of faith.
The serpent represents the curse.
Christ bears the curse.
The people looked at the symbol of what was killing them.
We look to the One who takes our sin upon Himself.
~Metaphor~
The pole in the wilderness becomes the cross on Calvary.
One temporary. One eternal. One pointing forward. One accomplishing salvation.
~Quote~
Athanasius:
“He became what we are, that He might make us what He is.”
Not that we become God. But that we are brought from death into life through Him.
~Application~ 
The question is not: Do you understand everything about the cross?
The question is: Have you looked? Have you trusted?
Have you stopped trying to save yourself and rested in Him?
The Meaning of “So” – How God Loves
And this is where we need to be careful as we step into the next verse.
Because we all know John 3:16: “For God so loved the world…”
And we instinctively hear that as: God loved the world so much.
Now that’s true. God’s love is immense.
But that’s not actually what the word “so” is doing there. 
¬ primarily HOW MUCH.
It means HOW. “In this way God loved the world…”
And what way is that?
Jesus has just told us: “As Moses lifted up the serpent… so must the Son of Man be lifted up…”
So when John 3:16 says:“For God so loved the world…”
It means:
God loved the world like this. By giving His Son to be lifted up.
How does a dying sinner live?
Numbers 21 answers:Look at what God has lifted up.
John 3 answers the same question.
So when Jesus moves into John 3:16, He is not changing topic.
He is explaining what that moment in the wilderness meant all along.
The “so” in John 3:16 tells us how God saves, not just how much He loves.
If you separate John 3:16 from John 3:14–15, you turn God’s love into a feeling.
But when you keep them together, you see that God’s love is a crucified Son lifted up for sinners. 

Conclusion: Only Two Responses
In that camp in the wilderness, there were only two kinds of people.
Those who were bitten and refused to look.
And those who were bitten and looked and lived.
That is still the divide.
Not moral and immoral.
 Not religious and irreligious.
But this:
Those who look to Christ.
 And those who don’t.
The gospel is not, Be better, try harder, more moral, understand more and live.
The gospel is: LOOK AN LIVE.
Christ has been lifted up. For sinners.For rebels.
   . For people who have SPOKEN AGAINST God, DOUBTED His goodness, tried to MANAGE their own lives.
That’s us 
And this is what John 3:16 is telling us.“For God so loved the world…”
This is how God loved the world.
Not by removing the wilderness.Not by removing the judgment.
But by giving His Son… to be lifted up.
“That whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life.”
Not perish. - That’s where the poison leads.
But life. That’s what God gives in His Son.
? Where are you looking?
       . At your performance?
      . At your past?
       .;At your effort?
Or at Christ? “Look to me, and be saved…” (Isaiah 45:22)
Stop trying to outgrow the poison.
Look!  And live.
