INTRODUCTION
Recap
Last week: 2 Samuel 7
David wanted to build God a house.
God said: "No David... I'll build you a house."
¬ cedar.  A dynasty.  A throne. A kingdom.
A Son.
.       "I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever."

But the Bible refuses to airbrush its heroes.

Today's journey:
Failure and Glory
A stained crown. A shining house.
Neither is enough. Both point to Christ.

From Promise to Problem
2 Sam 7 : Promise →  2 Sam 11 : Problem.
This pattern should feel familiar by now.
Adam… Noah… Abraham… Jacob… Judges… Saul… David...
Human failure. Divine faithfulness.
The Bible Arc has never been about perfect people.
It's about a perfect God keeping His promises.

{Read 2 Samuel 11:1–5...14–17...26–27}
The Stained Crown
Kings go to battle. “In the spring of the year, the time when KINGS GO OUT TO BATTLE, David sent Joab, and his servants with him, and all Israel. And they ravaged the Ammonites and besieged Rabbah. But DAVID REMAINED IN JERUSALEM

Small paragraph. Huge warning.
The king isn't where the king should be.
David sees. David sends.David takes.
Echoes Genesis 3.
Eve → Saw →Took.
David → Saw → Took.
Sin always grows.
Lust → Adultery → Deception → Murder → Concealment.

Interesting contrast…”“The ark and Israel and Judah dwell in booths, and my lord Joab and the servants of my lord are camping in the open field. Shall I then go to my house, to eat and to drink and to lie with my wife?


Uriah the Hittite: shows more covenant faithfulness
than David the king.

Ends with perhaps the saddest sentence in David's life.
.           "The thing David had done displeased the LORD."
Everyone else may have believed the cover story. God didn't.

Metaphor
The crown was meant to protect God's people.
Instead...it became stained.

Matthew Henry: Even the strongest saints, left to themselves, may fall into the worst sins.

~Application~
Don't trust yesterday's victories. Gifting.  ministry.
Trust Christ.

~Transition~
Thankfully...God loves David too much to leave him there.

{Read 2 Samuel 12:1–7...13}
The Mercy That Wounds
"The LORD sent Nathan..." = mercy

Doesn't feel like mercy. Feels like exposure, Conviction, Judgment.
But hidden sin is never safe.

Nathan tells a story →  David burns with anger.
Then… "You are the man."
Imagine the silence.
Everything changes.

The Bible often works like that.
We read about Pharaoh… then discover ourselves.
We read about Jonah… then discover ourselves.
We read about David… then discover ourselves.
The Bible isn't simply a window.
It's a mirror.

David finally says "I have sinned against the LORD."
No excuses, No blame, Just confession.

Psalm 51 sits behind this.
"Against You..."
Ultimately every sin is against God.

~Application~
Sometimes God's greatest kindness
is refusing to leave us comfortable.
Conviction hurts.
But it heals.

The Promise Survives
Nathan replies "The LORD has put away your sin."
Amazing grace.
David deserved death.
Instead… mercy.

But… forgiveness doesn't remove consequences.
The sword remains. Grace forgives.
Grace doesn't pretend sin was small.

Here's the important Arc point.
Why doesn't God's covenant collapse?
¬ cecause David is faithful.
Because God is. 

The covenant never rested on David's perfection.
It rested on God's promise.

Which raises the question...
If David isn't the final king...
Who is?

~Transition~
David wanted to build God's house. His son will.

{Read 1 Kings 8:10–13}

The Shining House
One of the greatest moments
in the Old Testament.
The ark enters. The cloud descends.
The priests can't stand. God fills the temple.

Think about the journey.
Garden →  Tabernacle → Temple → God dwelling with His people.

This isn't fake glory.
This is real.
Holy. Beautiful.

Yet… David's crown was stained.
Can a glorious building heal a sinful heart?

~Transition~ Solomon himself answers.

{Read 1 Kings 8:27–30}
The House Cannot Contain Him
Solomon surprises us.
He doesn't boast.He humbles himself.
"Heaven… cannot contain You.How much less this house."
Even on dedication day...the temple points beyond itself.

Listen carefully.
The repeated request in Solomon's prayer.
"Hear… and forgive."
That's the deepest need.
Not architecture.
Forgiveness.

~ Temple history~
Solomon's temple. Destroyed.
Second temple rebuilt. Ezra
Expanded by Herod.
Destroyed AD70.
Yet… the temple story isn't over.
Because before AD70
Jesus had already said "Destroy this temple..."
John says "He was speaking about His body."

~Transition~
Now we know where the story has been heading.

The Better King
David took. Christ gave.
David used power for himself.
Jesus used power to serve.

David sent Uriah to die. The Father sent His Son.

David's sin brought death.
Christ's death brings life.

Mark 10:45: “... For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many.”

That's the King we need.

The Greater Dwelling
John says "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us."
Literally tabernacled.
Jesus says Destroy this temple. Three days. Raise it again.John explains.
His body.

Temple becomes Person. – Meeting place becomes Christ.
Sacrifice becomes Christ, Priest becomes Christ, Glory becomes Christ.

Then something astonishing.
Those united to Christ become God's dwelling by the Spirit.
Church isn't replacing Christ, Church is unitedto Christ.

Final Hope
Revelation finishes where Genesis longed.
"The dwelling place of God is with man."
Then...
"No temple..."
Why?
Because the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are its temple.

The Arc finishes not with another building.
Not with another king, 
But with God Himself dwelling with His people.
Forever.
CONCLUSION
David's stained crown teaches us we needa better King.
Solomon's shining temple teaches us we needa greater dwelling.
Both point to Jesus.
He is the better David.
.     The greater Solomon.
.     The true Temple.
.     The final sacrifice.
.      The great High Priest.
.      God with us.

So don't leave here admiring David.Don't leave here admiring Solomon.
Leave here trusting Christ, because one day there will be no stained crowns.
No fallen kings. No ruined temples.
Only the Lord and the Lamb.
